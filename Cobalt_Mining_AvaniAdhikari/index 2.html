Cobalt: From Mines to Mobiles

How the plunder of the Democratic Republic of Congo powers the world.

Cobalt is a key component in Lithium-ion batteries, the most common energy-store used in commercial electronic products such as electric cars and mobile phones.

Lithium-ion batteries work by moving Lithium ions (a Lithium atom with one less electron) between a cathode (positively charged electrode) and an anode (negatively charged electrode). When the battery is charging, the ions move to the anode and when the battery is discharging, the ions move to the cathode. This back and forth movement of the ions generates electricity that powers our devices.

Cobalt is found in the cathode, helping to keep the battery neutral when the lithium ions move to the anode during recharging. By helping maintain neutrality during charging, cobalt ensures the stability of the battery. This stability is crucial for batteries to be long-lasting. Cobalt has high energy density—batteries with cobalt in their cathode hence can store more energy in a smaller space making our electric devices compact yet powerful.

Cobalt is able to achieve this due to its unique atomic structure as a transition metal. Though other transition metals such as nickel can be substituted for cobalt, they lack its stability and can catch fire during stress.

Without cobalt, our electronic devices would be more prone to overheating and catching fire. They would retain less power and would require to be charged more frequently. Most importantly, without cobalt, our batteries would not last as long as they do and would need to be replaced more frequently.

Today much of the world's cobalt comes from the Democratic Republic of Congo, a mineral rich region that has supported new technologies for the last 100 years at a very high human cost.

The DRC is one of the richest countries in terms of mineral wealth and its abundance has supported the world's technological developments for the last 100 years.

When King Leopold II began Belgium's brutal reign of the regions near the basin of the Congo River in 1885, he did so to control the region's supply of ivory and rubber.

By 1903, the "Congo Free State" was the largest supplier of rubber in the world, providing 5.9 thousand metric tons of rubber, just in time to support the European demand for automobile and bicycle tires.

A punch cartoon depicting King Leopold as snake attacking a rubber collector. Source: Wikipedia Commons

Though prices of rubber eventually fell due to competition form the Asian colonies, the discovery of its mineral wealth by the Belgians ensured that European interest in the region would continue.

Gold, Silver, and Diamond deposits in the region enriched the coffers of Belgium and England. Following the devastation of the First World War, European powers were keenly aware of the importance of Congo's wealth in their economic recovery, as highlighted by Louis Frank, the Belgian Minister of the Colonies in 1924:

"The valuable deposits are worked to their best economical possibilities , and with due care for the native population."

From the first cars to the first telegraph wires, new technology always brought new mineral interests to Congo.

With growing electrification increasing the demand for copper wiring, Copper became the country's leading export. Today, the DRC is the second largerst producer of Copper, behind Chile.

When the mushroom cloud fell on Hiroshima killing a hundred and fourty thousand civillians, it did so thanks to Congolese uranium.

The mine of Shinkolobwe provided uranium ores of such high grade for the nuclear age that it was called "a freak occurance in nature" by Colonel Nichols, an officer assigned to the Manhattan project.

For the next half century, access to this mine became a question of key geopolitical importance for anyone looking to establish their nuclear program while the local Congolese miners dealt with the effects of radioative exposure.

Most of Congo's mineral wealth is found in the south-east region of DRC known as Katanga

Alongside copper, gold, silver, and uranium the region also had deposits of tin, germanium, radium, zinc, lithium, cadmium, and manganese.

It is hence not surprising that control over Katanga remains a priority for politicians, militias, international conglomerates, and secret service agencies.

When Patrice Lumumba, Congo's first democratically elected leader, challenged Western mining interests, the mineral-rich province of Katanga was pushed into secession.

Subsequently, when Lumumba was overthrow in a US and Belgium backed coup, he was handed over to Belgian militias and Katangan secessionists in Elizabethville to be violently tortured and executed. The UN Peace keeping force stationed in the region alongside western media outlets just watched.

Today, Katanga finds itself again as an integral part of the global supply chain thanks to its large and abundant deposits of cobalt.

Somewhere around 30% of the world's total cobalt supply lies in this region, at depths so shallow that is can be extracted by anyone with a shovel.

While large indusrial sites run by Congolese and Chinese partnership dominate Katanga's formal mining sector, the region is also home to countless smaller, illegal, "artisanal mines".

It is in these "artisanal" mines where most of Katanga's men, women, and children work in extreme conditions, breathing toxic cobalt dust to provide the essential ingredient for the renewable transformation.

Though big tech companies claim to avoid artisanal mines in their cobalt sourcing, in practice, artisanal cobalt gets integrated to the "formal" supply chain via intermediaries.

Claims of independent audits too ring false—in a country where the last census was done in 1984, it is difficult to even obtain population estimates let alone a complete inventory of "artisinal" mines. As a result, it is impossible to have any cobalt exported from the DRC that is "ethically sourced".

Once the intermediaries have sold the artisinal cobalt ore to wholesale sellers, they undergo a first step processing in local industrial factories.

Cobalt processing factories operate under no environmental legislation and as a result often dump toxic by-products indescriminantly. Villagers living in the copper belts of Zambia and DRC often drink the water from polluted steams, and breathe toxic dust with 1000 times higher impurities than recommended by WHO.

Despite producing one of the most essential components for electrification, DRC does not have enough electricity to process its minerals in large scale. Only 19% of the country is electrified with rural areas lagging significantly behind at 2% electrification.

Lack of infrastructure in the DRC means that most of the actual cobalt refinement is done in facilities of the Developed World.

Around 65% of current cobalt refinement (mostly sourced from DRC) is done in China followed by Finland (11%) Belgium (5%) and Canada (5%). The few refinement facilities in DRC do not produce cobalt of sufficient high quality for industrial use and needs to be further processed abroad.

Cobalt's supply chain ends in factories that manufacture the electric goods that power our lives

From Asia to Europe to the US, cobalt from the DRC is used in Lithium-Ion batteries that are essential for all electric goods from cars to phones.

And with that, the Congolese people once again keep the world's newest technology running at the cost of their exploitation, as they have for the last 100 years.
